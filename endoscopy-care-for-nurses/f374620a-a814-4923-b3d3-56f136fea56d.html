Endoscopy Care for Nurses

Jason Seeks Help

Jason has been popping antacids for as long as he can remember. A high-pressure job, two kids in college, a big mortgage, and a passion for spicy foods had all conspired against him. He belched as he finished the last spoonful of his wife’s specialty, spicy chili with extra onions. The familiar acid started building in the back of his throat as he rose from the table to lumber over to his favorite recliner to watch television. Feeling his eyes begin to flutter shut several hours later, he struggled to get out of the chair and into the bedroom, the acid reflux bubbling up in the back of his throat as he rose.

Doctor’s Appointment
Jason had had enough and called his physician the next morning. He was directed to a gastroenterologist, for the long overdue colonoscopy he had been putting off for 3 years. He was also referred for the evaluation of what his primary care physician thought sounded a great deal like gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

Because Jason is 53 and had not had a colonoscopy, the gastroenterologist recommended the procedure as well as an upper endoscopy. The office staff calls Jason to give him the preparation instructions. He listened warily to the diet restrictions and preparation instructions required before the procedures:
  • Give up nuts.
  • No popcorn.
  • Stop taking your aspirin 7 days before the procedure.
  • Avoid high-fiber foods for 2 days before the colonoscopy.
  • Drink only clear liquids the day before.
  • Make sure to finish all the laxative drink the night before your procedure.
  • Be sure you have someone with you on the day of surgery.
Jason quietly wonders to himself, “What will I snack on? So, I have to practically purge my colon with a laxative? What if I forget to do part of the bowel prep?”

Getting Ready for the Procedure
It is 30 minutes before the scheduled procedures and Jason appears worried and restless as he rolls his thumbs and wiggles his toes under the sheet. The physician performing both procedures goes over the details once more and explains the risks, benefits, and alternatives. Jason quietly shakes his head “no” when asked if he has any questions.

The anxiety, fears, and worries build in Jason as the procedural start time inches closer.

It is in this fragile moment that you first meet Jason. Your assessment and reflection of the patient’s overall health is determined quickly and based on how they feel in this moment.

As nurses, we tend to focus on the clinical task at hand and we may overlook the personal impact that preparing for endoscopies has on the patient, especially procedures that require bowel preps.

How often have you considered how much the patient’s life has been disrupted before an endoscopy procedure?

You are responsible for verifying that the consent is signed, dated, and matches with the ordered procedure (Rothrock, 2022). Remember, you serve as a witness that the patient or their legal representative signed the consent. You are not the person obtaining informed consent from the patient, as this is within the physician’s role. Although, it is reasonable for you to bring concerns of the patient to the physician’s attention, as it is your duty to be their advocate.
If sedation will be used, identify the responsible adult escorting the patient home after the procedure. Provide procedural-related education and expectations to the patient and their support person. Insert an IV line and run IV fluids and antibiotics as ordered by the physician. Ensure that the patient removes any assistive devices (e.g., contacts, dentures, partial plates), jewelry, and piercings before the procedure. Assess if the patient has any implants, including the location and type.
Sedation and Pain Relief
Sedation in the endoscopy setting is used to help make the procedure more comfortable and tolerable for the patient. Sedatives can be used to facilitate amnesia and a decreased level of consciousness, and analgesics can be used to facilitate pain relief. It is safer to sedate patients at a moderate sedation level at which they maintain their protective reflexes.
Minimal or moderate sedation is appropriate for most non-advanced endoscopic procedures. Recall that during minimal sedation, the patient responds normally to verbal commands and cardiovascular and ventilatory functions are unaffected (Umran & Kaye, 2017). Their cognitive function and coordination may be impaired.
During moderate sedation (previously called conscious sedation), patients respond purposefully to verbal commands, either alone or with light tactile stimulation (Umran & Kaye, 2017). Cardiovascular function is usually maintained. No interventions are needed to maintain the patient’s airway.
Monitor for indications that the patient has advanced to a state of deep sedation, such as the patient becomes unarousable and only responds purposefully to repeated or painful stimulation. If this occurs, they may need intervention to maintain their airway (e.g., jaw thrusts, oral/nasal airway) but cardiovascular function is typically maintained.
The goals of sedation for GI endoscopy are to (Cohen, 2023):

  • Relieve patient anxiety and pain.

  • Improve procedural outcomes.

  • Reduce the patient's memory of the event.
Scope of Practice
State boards of nursing define the nurse’s scope of practice related to moderate sedation in varying and sometimes ambiguous ways (Eslinger, n.d.). The Joint Commission requires organizations to define how they will determine qualifications to administer sedation but requires that the person be qualified to manage the patient at whatever level of sedation or anesthesia is reached intentionally or unintentionally. This includes:
  • Managing a compromised airway
  • Handling a compromised cardiovascular system
  • Evaluating patients before sedation is administered
  • Rescuing patients who slip into a deeper-than-desired level of sedation
Per the ASGE and the Society of Gastroenterology Nurses and Associates (SGNA) (2017) guidelines, a registered nurse who has been appropriately trained can administer medications for minimal and moderate sedation in stages while under the supervision of an endoscopist. The nurse's primary responsibility is to monitor the patient's response to the sedatives and analgesics. However, the RN can also perform short, interruptible tasks in addition to monitoring the patient, but they should never leave the head of the bed.
If a deeper level of sedation is desired, the monitoring nurse must be adequately trained to manage sedation-related adverse events and have no other function except to observe and monitor the patient (SGNA, 2017; Early et al., 2018). If necessary, another nurse or nursing assistant may perform the other tasks, which may include:
  • Repositioning the patient during the procedure
  • The application of abdominal pressure
  • Assisting with therapeutic interventions, such as polypectomy and hemostasis
Reassurance and emotional support throughout the procedure will also help ensure the patient’s cooperation.
The ASGE recommends sedation be administered by an anesthesia provider when the following factors are present (Early et al., 2018):
  • Elevated risk for airway complications
  • History of multiple or severe comorbidities
  • Complex endoscopic procedures
  • Deeper levels of sedation are required
  • Intolerance to standard sedatives

The Association of periOperative Registered Nurses (AORN) (2021) recommends that perioperative RNs who administer moderate sedation or analgesia be directly supervised by an anesthesia professional, such as:
  • Anesthesiologist
  • Certified registered nurse anesthetist
  • Qualified licensed independent practitioner (e.g., surgeon, endoscopist)
Monitoring
During endoscopic procedures, patients must be monitored for changes in vital signs and sedation levels. This is done through visual observation and monitoring devices. Vital signs should be taken at minimum every 5 minutes throughout the procedure as well as before and after sedative analgesics. Vitals are reassessed during recovery and before discharge (Cohen, 2023).
Hemodynamic monitoring is crucial, including continuous heart rate and blood pressure checks (Cohen, 2023). Oxygenation is usually assessed with pulse oximetry to detect hypoxemia. Electrocardiographic (ECG) monitoring is necessary for patients with cardiovascular disease or cardiac dysrhythmias. Continuous pulse oximetry is required but may not detect early hypoventilation. Capnography is used for ventilation monitoring and helps detect apnea and airway obstruction.
With adequate monitoring equipment, complications can be minimized and the overall safety of endoscopy procedures improved (Mahmud & Berzin, 2016).
Medications
For minimal and moderate sedation during endoscopies, a combination of benzodiazepines and narcotic analgesics is typically used (Cohen, 2023; Early et al., 2018). A combination of fentanyl (Duragesic®) and midazolam (Versed®) is most often used to reduce anxiety and pain during endoscopy procedures.
The benzodiazepines midazolam and diazepam (Valium®) are commonly used and have comparable effectiveness. However, midazolam is preferred for its fast onset, short duration, lower risk of thrombophlebitis, and amnestic properties. Midazolam can be used alone for older adults or patients at risk for adverse events. Dosages depend on age and comorbidities.
Opiates like meperidine (Demerol®) and fentanyl can also be used for sedation and pain relief, with fentanyl having a more rapid onset and lower incidence of nausea. Fentanyl is given in small intermittent boluses and should be used cautiously with lower doses for patients with a history of seizures. Meperidine is rarely used due to longer recovery times and adverse effects. Antagonists like naloxone (Narcan®) and flumazenil (Romazicon®) should be available in case of adverse reactions.
Propofol (Diprivan®) is gaining widespread use as a safe and effective anesthetic agent in the endoscopy suite. It reduces the time between admission and discharge as it shortens the recovery period. Patients who were anesthetized with propofol appeared better sedated and demonstrated higher postanesthesia recovery scores without any increase in post-procedural complication rates (Nishizawa & Suzuki, 2018).
Diphenhydramine (Benadryl®), promethazine (Phenergan®), and droperidol (Inapsine®) can be added to potentiate the sedative effects, but droperidol should only be used as a last resort due to potential heart-related risks (Early et al., 2018).
Communication
For procedures where the patient is expected to be unable to communicate verbally (e.g., bronchoscopy), establish a means of communication, such as thumbs up for “yes” and thumbs down for “no.” A means of communicating pain level is also important. Educate the patient and practice communication techniques BEFORE initiating sedation. From beginning to end, keep the patient informed about what steps in the procedure are happening. Use light touch and verbal reassurance.
Complications From Sedation
A number of complications related to sedation may be seen in patients undergoing endoscopic procedures (Hormati et al., 2019):

  • Bradycardia
  • Hypotension
  • Hypoxia
  • Dysrhythmias
  • Aspiration
  • Allergic reactions

Bradycardia

  • Bradycardia may occur at any time, so it is vital to check the ECG monitor frequently. If the heart rate slows, the procedure may have to be stopped for a few seconds; if the heart rate does not improve, the patient may require atropine.

Hypotension

  • Hypotension is often seen due to the use of medications. In most cases, it can be reversed with a bolus of fluids. Thus, extra IV fluids should always be available.

Hypoxia

  • Hypoxia may occur from respiratory depression. It is a serious complication and should be identified immediately. In most cases, increasing oxygen and stopping all sedation may be effective. In some cases, the patient may need a face mask with a higher concentration of oxygen. If the patient fails to respond, intubation and mechanical ventilation are required. Staff must remain prepared to administer advanced cardiac life-support.

Dysrhythmias

  • Dysrhythmias may occur, usually as a result of hypoxia. Never assume that the dysrhythmia is benign until the vital signs have been assessed and the pulse oximeter checked. If the dysrhythmia persists, it is best to abort the procedure and obtain a cardiology consultation.

Aspiration

  • Aspiration is always a concern when the procedure is done under sedation, so most surgeons will attempt to elevate the head slightly on a pillow. A suction catheter should be available in case the patient has excess drooling of saliva.

Allergic reactions

  • Allergic reactions can occur from medications, but they are rare if the surgeon has done a thorough history and physical exam.

Postoperative Care

After an endoscopy, monitor the patient as the sedative wears off. They may feel tired or have trouble concentrating. Bloating and a sore throat are common but temporary side effects. Breastfeeding moms may need to pump and discard milk.
Instruct the patient not to drive or work that day. Inform them to seek medical attention if they experience any of these signs and symptoms:
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • A firm abdomen
  • Vomiting
  • Fever
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Severe throat pain
  • A crunching feeling in the neck
  • Red or black colors in vomit or stool
Discharge
Endoscopy units may have certain criteria for patient discharge, such as being home-ready, awake, oriented, and having stable vital signs. The patient's discharge may also be based on pre-approved clinical criteria and scoring methodologies during the discharge assessment (Early et al., 2018). Before discharge, the IV line should be removed, and written discharge instructions should be provided to the patient and their support system for follow-up care.

Expanding Your Knowledge

Knowledge of the appropriate cleaning, disinfection, and sterilization of equipment is equally as important as being conversant with its use. Multiple professional organizations, including ASGE, the SGNA, and AORN have developed evidence-based guidelines for the safe reprocessing of GI endoscopes. It is the clinician’s role to stringently adhere to these standards and guidelines to maintain a safe and infection-free endoscopy suite.

Key Takeaways

  • Minimal or moderate sedation is appropriate for most non-advanced procedures, with the goal of relieving patient anxiety and pain, improving procedural outcomes, and reducing the patient's memory of the event.
  • A combination of fentanyl and midazolam is commonly used to reduce anxiety and pain and the patient is monitored for changes in vital signs and sedation levels.
  • Nurses have a set of responsibilities in sedation-related procedures, including managing a compromised airway, evaluating patients before sedation is administered, and rescuing patients who slip into a deeper-than-desired level of sedation.
